Community-level composting in a rural area in Germany

**Compost** is a mixture of ingredients used as plant fertilizer and to improve [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil") 's physical, chemical, and biological properties. It is commonly prepared by [decomposing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition") plant and food waste, recycling organic materials, and manure. The resulting mixture is rich in plant nutrients and [beneficial organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beneficial_organism "Beneficial organism"), such as bacteria, protozoa, nematodes, and fungi. Compost improves soil fertility in [gardens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden "Garden"), [landscaping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landscaping "Landscaping"), [horticulture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horticulture "Horticulture"), [urban agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_agriculture "Urban agriculture"), and [organic farming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_farming "Organic farming"), reducing dependency on commercial chemical fertilizers.[^1] The benefits of compost include providing nutrients to crops as [fertilizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilizer "Fertilizer"), acting as a [soil conditioner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_conditioner "Soil conditioner"), increasing the [humus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humus "Humus") or [humic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humic_acids "Humic acids") contents of the soil, and introducing beneficial microbes that help to suppress pathogens in the soil and reduce soil-borne diseases.

At the simplest level, composting requires gathering a mix of [green waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_waste "Green waste") (nitrogen-rich materials such as leaves, grass, and food scraps) and [brown waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_waste "Brown waste") (woody materials rich in carbon, such as stalks, paper, and wood chips).[^1] The materials break down into humus in a process taking months.[^2] Composting can be a multistep, closely monitored process with measured inputs of water, air, and carbon- and nitrogen-rich materials. The [decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition") process is aided by shredding the plant matter, adding water, and ensuring proper aeration by regularly turning the mixture in a process using open piles or [windrows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windrow "Windrow").[^1] [^3] [Fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"), [earthworms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthworm "Earthworm"), and other [detritivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detritivore "Detritivore") further break up the organic material. [Aerobic bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_bacteria "Aerobic bacteria") and fungi manage the chemical process by converting the inputs into heat, [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), and [ammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium") ions.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Composter_%28compost_bin%29_made_from_hollow_log_%28K%C3%B5rvemaa%2C_Estonia%2C_2023%29.png/250px-Composter_%28compost_bin%29_made_from_hollow_log_%28K%C3%B5rvemaa%2C_Estonia%2C_2023%29.png)

Composter made from a hollow log

Composting is an important part of waste management, since food and other compostable materials make up about 20% of waste in landfills, and due to anaerobic conditions, these materials take longer to biodegrade in the landfill.[^4] [^5] Composting offers an environmentally superior alternative to using organic material for landfill because composting reduces [methane emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_emissions "Methane emissions") due to anaerobic conditions, and provides economic and environmental co-benefits.[^6] [^7] For example, compost can also be used for land and stream reclamation, wetland construction, and landfill cover.

## Fundamentals

Composting is an [aerobic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aerobic "wikt:aerobic") method of decomposing organic solid wastes,[^8] so it can be used to recycle organic material. The process involves decomposing organic material into a humus-like material, known as compost, which is a good fertilizer for plants.

Composting organisms require four equally important ingredients to work effectively:[^3]

- **Carbon** is needed for energy; the microbial [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") of carbon produces the heat required for other parts of the composting process.[^3] High carbon materials tend to be brown and dry.[^1] [^3]
- **Nitrogen** is needed to grow and reproduce more organisms to oxidize the carbon.[^3] High nitrogen materials tend to be green [^1] and wet.[^3] They can also include colourful fruits and vegetables.[^1]
- **Oxygen** is required for oxidizing the carbon, the decomposition process.[^3] Aerobic bacteria need oxygen levels above 5% to perform the processes needed for composting.[^3]
- **Water** is necessary in the right amounts to maintain activity without causing locally [anaerobic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anaerobic "wikt:anaerobic") conditions.[^1] [^3]

Certain ratios of these materials allow microorganisms to work at a rate that will heat up the compost pile. Active management of the pile (e.g., turning over the compost heap) is needed to maintain sufficient oxygen and the right moisture level. The air/water balance is critical to maintaining high temperatures 130–160 °F (54–71 °C) until the materials are broken down.[^9]

Composting is most efficient with a [carbon-to-nitrogen ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-to-nitrogen_ratio "Carbon-to-nitrogen ratio") of about 25:1.[^10] [Hot composting](#Hot_and_cold_composting_–_impact_on_timing) focuses on retaining heat to increase the decomposition rate, thus producing compost more quickly. Rapid composting is favored by having a carbon-to-nitrogen ratio of about 30 carbon units or less. Above 30, the substrate is nitrogen starved. Below 15, it is likely to outgas a portion of nitrogen as ammonia.[^11]

Nearly all dead plant and animal materials have both carbon and nitrogen in different amounts.[^12] Fresh grass clippings have an average ratio of about 15:1 and dry autumn leaves about 50:1 depending upon species.[^3] Composting is an ongoing and dynamic process; adding new sources of carbon and nitrogen consistently, as well as active management, is important.

### Organisms

Organisms can break down organic matter in compost if provided with the correct mixture of water, oxygen, carbon, and nitrogen.[^3] They fall into two broad categories: chemical decomposers, which perform chemical processes on the organic waste, and physical decomposers, which process the waste into smaller pieces through methods such as grinding, tearing, chewing, and digesting.[^3]

#### Chemical decomposers

- [Bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") are the most abundant and important of all the [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") found in compost.[^3] Bacteria process carbon and nitrogen and excrete plant-available nutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and magnesium.[^3] Depending on the phase of composting, [mesophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesophile "Mesophile") or [thermophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermophile "Thermophile") bacteria may be the most prominent.
	- Mesophilic bacteria get compost to the thermophilic stage through oxidation of organic material.[^3] Afterwards they cure it, which makes the fresh compost more bioavailable for plants.[^3] [^13]
		- Thermophilic bacteria do not reproduce and are not active between −5 and 25 °C (23 and 77 °F),[^14] yet are found throughout soil. They activate once the mesophilic bacteria have begun to break down organic matter and increase the temperature to their optimal range.[^13] They have been shown to enter soils via rainwater.[^13] They are present so broadly because of many factors, including their spores being resilient.[^15] Thermophilic bacteria thrive at higher temperatures, reaching 40–60 °C (104–140 °F) in typical mixes. Large-scale composting operations, such as [windrow composting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windrow_composting "Windrow composting"), may exceed this temperature, potentially killing beneficial soil microorganisms but also [pasteurizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasteurizing "Pasteurizing") the waste.[^13]
		- [Actinomycetota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actinomycetota "Actinomycetota") are needed to break down paper products such as newspaper, [bark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bark_\(botany\) "Bark (botany)"), etc., and other large molecules such as lignin and cellulose that are more difficult to decompose.[^3] The "pleasant, earthy smell of compost" is attributed to Actinomycetota.[^3] They make carbon, ammonia, and nitrogen nutrients available to plants.[^3]
- [Fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi") such as [molds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mold_\(fungus\) "Mold (fungus)") and [yeasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeast "Yeast") help break down materials that bacteria cannot, especially cellulose and [lignin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lignin "Lignin") in woody material.[^3]
- [Protozoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protozoa "Protozoa") contribute to biodegradation of organic matter and consume inactive bacteria, fungi, and micro-organic particulates.[^16]

#### Physical decomposers

- [Ants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant "Ant") create nests, making the soil more porous and transporting nutrients to different areas of the compost.[^3]
- Beetles such as [grubs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grub_\(larva\) "Grub (larva)") feed on decaying vegetables.[^3]
- [Earthworms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthworm "Earthworm") ingest partly composted material and excrete worm castings,[^3] making nitrogen, calcium, phosphorus, and magnesium available to plants.[^3] The tunnels they create as they move through the compost also increase aeration and drainage.[^3]
- [Flies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fly "Fly") feed on almost all organic material and put bacteria into the compost.[^3] Their population is kept in check by [mites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mite "Mite") and the thermophilic temperatures that are unsuitable for fly larvae.[^3]
- [Millipedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millipede "Millipede") break down plant material.[^3]
- [Rotifers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotifer "Rotifer") feed on plant particles.[^3]
- [Snails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snail "Snail") and [slugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slug "Slug") feed on living or fresh plant material.[^3] They should be removed from compost before use, as they can damage plants and crops.[^3]
- [Sow bugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sow_bugs "Sow bugs") feed on rotting wood and decaying vegetation.[^3]
- [Springtails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Springtail "Springtail") feed on fungi, molds, and decomposing plants.[^3]

### Phases of composting

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Komposzt%C3%A1l%C3%B3.JPG/250px-Komposzt%C3%A1l%C3%B3.JPG)

Three year old household compost

Under ideal conditions, composting proceeds through three major phases:[^16] [^17]

1. **Mesophilic phase:** The initial, mesophilic phase is when the decomposition is carried out under moderate temperatures by [mesophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesophile "Mesophile") microorganisms. 2 to 8 days
2. **Thermophilic phase:** As the temperature rises, a second, thermophilic phase starts, in which various [thermophilic bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermophilic_bacteria "Thermophilic bacteria") carry out the decomposition under higher temperatures (50 to 60 °C (122 to 140 °F).)
3. **Cooling phase** (also called Mesophilic II)
4. **Maturation phase:** As the supply of high-energy compounds dwindles, the temperature starts to decrease.

Semicomposting is the degradation process that handles volumes of organic waste lower than that recommended for composting and therefore does not present a [thermophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermophilic "Thermophilic") stage, because [mesophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesophilic "Mesophilic") microorganisms are the only responsible ones, for the degradation of [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter").[^18] [^19]

### Hot and cold composting – impact on timing

The time required to compost material relates to the volume of material, the particle size of the inputs (e.g. [wood chips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood_chips "Wood chips") break down faster than branches), and the amount of mixing and aeration.[^3] Generally, larger piles reach higher temperatures and remain in a thermophilic stage for days or weeks. This is hot composting and is the usual method for large-scale municipal facilities and agricultural operations.

The Berkeley method produces finished compost in 18 days. It requires assembly of at least 1 cubic metre (35 cu ft) of material at the outset and needs turning every two days after an initial four-day phase.[^20] Such short processes involve some changes to traditional methods, including smaller, more homogenized particle sizes in the input materials, controlling carbon-to-nitrogen ratio (C:N) at 30:1 or less, and careful monitoring of the moisture level.

Cold composting is a slower process that can take up to a year to complete.[^21] It results from smaller piles, including many residential compost piles that receive small amounts of kitchen and garden waste over extended periods. Piles smaller than 1 cubic metre (35 cu ft) tend not to reach and maintain high temperatures.[^22] Turning is not necessary with cold composting, although a risk exists that parts of the pile may go anaerobic as it becomes compacted or waterlogged.

### Pathogen removal

Composting can destroy some [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen") and [seeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seed "Seed"), by reaching certain temperatures. Dealing with stabilized compost – i.e. composted material in which microorganisms have finished digesting the organic matter and the temperature has reached between 50 and 70 °C (122 and 158 °F) – poses very little risk, as these temperatures kill pathogens and even make [oocysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oocysts "Oocysts") unviable.[^23] The temperature at which a pathogen dies depends on the pathogen, how long the temperature is maintained (seconds to weeks), and pH.[^24]

Compost products such as compost tea and compost extracts have been found to have an inhibitory effect on *[Fusarium oxysporum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusarium_oxysporum "Fusarium oxysporum")*, [*Rhizoctonia* species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizoctonia_solani "Rhizoctonia solani"), and *[Pythium debaryanum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythium_debaryanum "Pythium debaryanum"),* plant pathogens that can cause crop diseases.[^25] Aerated compost teas are more effective than compost extracts.[^25] The [microbiota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiota "Microbiota") and enzymes present in compost extracts also have a suppressive effect on fungal plant pathogens.[^26] Compost is a good source of [biocontrol agents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocontrol "Biocontrol") like [*B. subtilis*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacillus_subtilis "Bacillus subtilis"), *B. licheniformis,* and P. *chrysogenum* that fight plant pathogens.[^25] Sterilizing the compost, compost tea, or compost extracts reduces the effect of pathogen suppression.[^25]

### Diseases that can be contracted from handling compost

When turning compost that has not gone through phases where temperatures above 50 °C (122 °F) are reached, a mouth mask and gloves must be worn to protect from diseases that can be contracted from handling compost, including:[^27]

- [Aspergillosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspergillosis "Aspergillosis")
- [Farmer's lung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farmer%27s_lung "Farmer's lung")
- [Histoplasmosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histoplasmosis "Histoplasmosis") – a fungus that grows in guano and bird droppings
- [Legionnaires' disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legionnaires%27_disease "Legionnaires' disease")
- [Paronychia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paronychia "Paronychia") – via infection around the fingernails and toenails
- [Tetanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetanus "Tetanus") – a central nervous system disease

[Oocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oocyte "Oocyte") are rendered unviable by temperatures over 50 °C (122 °F).[^23]

## Environmental benefits

Compost adds organic matter to the soil and increases the nutrient content and biodiversity of microbes in soil.[^28] Composting at home reduces the amount of green waste being hauled to dumps or composting facilities. The reduced volume of materials being picked up by trucks results in fewer trips, which in turn lowers the overall emissions from the waste-management fleet.

## Materials that can be composted

Potential sources of compostable materials, or feedstocks, include residential, agricultural, and commercial waste streams. Residential food or yard waste can be composted at home,[^29] or collected for inclusion in a large-scale municipal composting facility. In some regions, it could also be included in a local or neighborhood composting project.[^30] [^31]

### Organic solid waste

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Spontaneous_combustion_of_compost_pile.jpg/250px-Spontaneous_combustion_of_compost_pile.jpg)

A large compost pile is steaming with the heat generated by thermophilic microorganisms.

The two broad categories of organic solid waste are green and brown. Green waste is generally considered a source of nitrogen and includes pre- and post-consumer [food waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_waste "Food waste"), grass clippings, garden trimmings, and fresh leaves.[^1] Animal carcasses, roadkill, and butcher residue can also be composted, and these are considered nitrogen sources.[^32]

Brown waste is a carbon source. Typical examples are dried vegetation and woody material such as fallen leaves, straw, woodchips, limbs, logs, pine needles, sawdust, and wood ash, but not charcoal ash.[^1] [^33] Products derived from wood such as paper and plain cardboard are also considered carbon sources.[^1]

### Animal manure and bedding

On many farms, the basic composting ingredients are animal [manure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manure "Manure") generated on the farm as a [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") source, and bedding as the carbon source. Straw and sawdust are common bedding materials. Nontraditional bedding materials are also used, including newspaper and chopped cardboard.[^1] The amount of manure composted on a livestock farm is often determined by cleaning schedules, land availability, and weather conditions. Each type of manure has its own physical, chemical, and biological characteristics. Cattle and horse manures, when mixed with bedding, possess good qualities for composting. Swine manure, which is very wet and usually not mixed with bedding material, must be mixed with straw or similar raw materials. Poultry manure must be blended with high-carbon, low-nitrogen materials.[^34]

### Human excreta

[Human excreta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_excreta "Human excreta"), sometimes called "humanure" in the composting context,[^35] [^36] can be added as an input to the composting process since it is a nutrient-rich organic material. Nitrogen, which serves as a building block for important plant amino acids, is found in solid human waste.[^37] [^38] Phosphorus, which helps plants convert sunlight into energy in the form of ATP, can be found in liquid human waste.[^39] [^40]

Solid human waste can be collected directly in [composting toilets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composting_toilets "Composting toilets"), or indirectly in the form of [sewage sludge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_sludge "Sewage sludge") after it has undergone treatment in a [sewage treatment plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_treatment "Sewage treatment"). Both processes require capable design, as potential health risks need to be managed. In the case of home composting, a wide range of microorganisms, including bacteria, viruses, and parasitic worms, can be present in feces, and improper processing can pose significant health risks.[^41] In the case of large sewage treatment facilities that collect wastewater from a range of residential, commercial and industrial sources, there are additional considerations. The composted sewage sludge, referred to as [biosolids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosolids "Biosolids"), can be contaminated with a variety of metals and pharmaceutical compounds.[^42] [^43] Insufficient processing of biosolids can also lead to problems when the material is applied to land.[^44]

[Urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine "Urine") can be put on compost piles or directly used as fertilizer.[^45] Adding urine to compost can increase temperatures, so can increase its ability to destroy pathogens and unwanted seeds. Unlike feces, urine does not attract disease-spreading flies (such as [houseflies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housefly "Housefly") or [blowflies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calliphoridae "Calliphoridae")), and it does not contain the most hardy of pathogens, such as [parasitic worm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasitic_worm "Parasitic worm") eggs.[^46]

### Animal remains

Animal carcasses may be composted as a disposal option. Such material is rich in nitrogen.[^47]

### Human bodies

[Human composting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_composting "Human composting") (called natural organic reduction in legal codes and regulations [^48] [^49]) is a process for [the final disposition of human remains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disposal_of_human_corpses "Disposal of human corpses") in which [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbes "Microbes") and organic material convert a deceased body into compost. Developed in the early 21st century as an environmentally sustainable alternative to burial and cremation, human composting is legally sanctioned in several U.S states and Sweden. Human composting has prompted much debate around environmental impacts, legalization, and religious considerations.[^50] [^51] [^52]

## Composting technologies

Backyard composter

### Industrial-scale composting

#### In-vessel composting

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/In-vessel_composter.jpg/250px-In-vessel_composter.jpg)

In-vessel composter using a blower and auger to aerate and mix the material

[In-vessel composting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In-vessel_composting "In-vessel composting") generally describes a group of methods that confine the [composting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composting "Composting") materials within a building, container, or vessel.[^53] In-vessel composting systems can consist of metal or plastic tanks or concrete bunkers in which air flow and temperature can be controlled, using the principles of a " [bioreactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioreactor "Bioreactor") ". Generally the air circulation is metered in via buried tubes that allow fresh air to be injected under pressure, with the exhaust being extracted through a [biofilter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biofilter "Biofilter"), with temperature and moisture conditions monitored using probes in the mass to allow maintenance of optimum [aerobic decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_decomposition "Aerobic decomposition") conditions. Many In-vessel composters also use augers or rotation to agitate the material, speeding up degradation by continuously homogenizing and mechanically aerating the material.[^54] Common examples of this include rotary drum composters from manufacturers like XACT [^55] and auger based in-vessel technology like the Earth Flow from Green Mountain Technologies [^56] and Biomax G by Sorain Cecchinni.[^57]

In-vessel composting is generally used for putrescible municipal scale [organic waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_waste "Organic waste") processing, including final treatment of sewage [biosolids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosolids "Biosolids"), food waste, or the organic fraction of municipal solid waste. In-vessel composting allows for sanitization of pathogens so the waste can be reclaimed as a soil amendment, while minimizing the environmental impact in the form of odors, runoff or emissions. In-vessel composting can also refer to [aerated static pile composting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerated_static_pile_composting "Aerated static pile composting") within a building or under removable covers that enclose the piles, as with the system in extensive use by farmer groups in Thailand, supported by the National Science and Technology Development Agency there.[^58] In recent years, smaller scale in-vessel composting has been advanced. These often use custom or common roll-off waste dumpsters as the vessel. They may also use retrofitted shipping containers or custom drums.

#### Aerated static-pile composting

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Aerated_Static_Pile_%28ASP%29_Composting.jpg/250px-Aerated_Static_Pile_%28ASP%29_Composting.jpg)

Aerated Static Pile compost facility located in the City of Hutchinson

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Aerated_Static_Pile_%28ASP%29_Compost.jpg/250px-Aerated_Static_Pile_%28ASP%29_Compost.jpg)

Image showing the blowers located behind the push wall in the above image

[Aerated static pile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerated_static_pile_composting "Aerated static pile composting") (ASP) composting refers to systems using forced aeration to keep compost material aerobic without physical manipulation. This is typically done by placing mixed feedstocks on perforated piping where air is then either pushed up (positive aeration), sucked down (negative aeration), or pushed and pulled (reversing aeration) through the material. If air is pulled through the material (negative and reversing aeration) it is typically then run through a [scrubber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scrubber "Scrubber") like a [biofilter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biofilter "Biofilter") to remove odor and emissions.[^59]

ASP's are typically constructed outdoors in mass beds or bunker configurations (see image for example of bunker style ASP). They can also be constructed within open-sided buildings, enclosed bunkers known as tunnels, or [windrows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windrow_composting "Windrow composting"). Many ASPs are protected from rain and precipitation with a roof, reducing leachate generation and allowing operators to evaporate detained water through [biodrying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodrying "Biodrying").

#### Windrow composting

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/CVRD7windrow.turner.jpg/250px-CVRD7windrow.turner.jpg)

Windrow turner used on maturing piles at a biosolids composting facility in Canada.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/CVRD6windrow.matureing.jpg/250px-CVRD6windrow.matureing.jpg)

Maturing windrows at an in-vessel composting facility.

In [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), [windrow composting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windrow_composting "Windrow composting") is the production of compost by piling [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") or [biodegradable waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodegradable_waste "Biodegradable waste"), such as animal manure and crop residues, in long rows – [windrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windrow "Windrow").

As the process is aerobic, it is also known as Open Windrow Composting (OWC) or Open Air Windrow Composting (OAWC).[^60]

### Other systems at household level

#### Hügelkultur (raised garden beds or mounds)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/End_point_%284315712587%29.jpg/250px-End_point_%284315712587%29.jpg)

An almost completed hügelkultur bed; the bed does not have soil on it yet.

The practice of making raised garden beds or mounds filled with rotting wood is also called *[Hügelkultur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%BCgelkultur "Hügelkultur")* in German.[^61] [^62] It is in effect creating a [nurse log](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse_log "Nurse log") that is covered with soil.

Benefits of *Hügelkultur* garden beds include water retention and warming of soil.[^61] [^63] Buried wood acts like a [sponge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sponge_\(material\) "Sponge (material)") as it decomposes, able to capture water and store it for later use by crops planted on top of the bed.[^61] [^64]

#### Composting toilets

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Compost_toilet.jpg/250px-Compost_toilet.jpg)

Composting toilet at Activism Festival 2010 in the mountains outside Jerusalem

A [composting toilet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composting_toilet "Composting toilet") is a type of [dry toilet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_toilet "Dry toilet") that treats [human waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_waste "Human waste") by a biological process called composting. This process leads to the [decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition") of [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") and turns human waste into compost-like material. Composting is carried out by microorganisms (mainly [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus")) under controlled [aerobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_organism "Aerobic organism") conditions.[^65] Most composting toilets use no water for flushing and are therefore called " [dry toilets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_toilet "Dry toilet") ".

In many composting toilet designs, a carbon additive such as [sawdust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sawdust "Sawdust"), [coconut coir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coir "Coir"), or [peat moss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphagnum "Sphagnum") is added after each use. This practice creates air pockets in the human waste to promote aerobic decomposition. This also improves the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio and reduces potential [odor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odor "Odor"). Most composting toilet systems rely on [mesophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesophile "Mesophile") composting. Longer retention time in the composting chamber also facilitates [pathogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen") die-off. The end product can also be moved to a secondary system – usually another composting step – to allow more time for mesophilic composting to further reduce pathogens.

Composting toilets, together with the secondary composting step, produce a [humus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humus "Humus") -like end product that can be used to enrich soil if local regulations allow this. Some composting toilets have [urine diversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine_diversion "Urine diversion") systems in the toilet bowl to collect the urine separately and control excess moisture. A [vermifilter toilet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vermifilter_toilet "Vermifilter toilet") is a composting toilet with flushing water where [earthworms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthworm "Earthworm") are used to promote decomposition to compost.

- [Vermicompost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vermicompost "Vermicompost") (also called [worm castings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worm_cast "Worm cast"), worm humus, worm manure, or worm faeces) is the end product of the breakdown of [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") by earthworms.[^66] These castings have been shown to contain reduced levels of contaminants and a higher saturation of nutrients than the organic materials before vermicomposting.[^67]
- [Black soldier fly (*Hermetia illucens*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermetia_illucens "Hermetia illucens") larvae are able to rapidly consume large amounts of organic material and can be used to treat human waste. The resulting compost still contains nutrients and can be used for [biogas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogas "Biogas") production, or further traditional composting or vermicomposting [^68] [^69]
- [Bokashi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bokashi_\(horticulture\) "Bokashi (horticulture)") is a fermentation process rather than a decomposition process, and so retains the feedstock's energy, nutrient and carbon contents. There must be sufficient carbohydrate for fermentation to complete and therefore the process is typically applied to food waste, including noncompostable items. Carbohydrate is transformed into lactic acid, which dissociates naturally to form lactate, a biological energy carrier. The preserved result is therefore readily consumed by soil microbes and from there by the entire soil food web, leading to a significant increase in soil organic carbon and turbation. The process completes in weeks and returns soil acidity to normal.
- Co-composting is a technique that processes organic solid waste together with other input materials such as dewatered [fecal sludge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fecal_sludge "Fecal sludge") or [sewage sludge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_sludge "Sewage sludge").[^10]
- [Anaerobic digestion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion "Anaerobic digestion") combined with mechanical sorting of mixed waste streams is increasingly being used in developed countries due to regulations controlling the amount of organic matter allowed in landfills. Treating [biodegradable waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodegradable_waste "Biodegradable waste") before it enters a landfill reduces [global warming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming "Global warming") from fugitive [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"); untreated waste breaks down anaerobically in a landfill, producing [landfill gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landfill_gas "Landfill gas") that contains methane, a potent [greenhouse gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"). The methane produced in an anaerobic digester can be used as [biogas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogas "Biogas").[^70]
- Small-scale and organic farms can also produce aerobic compost piles for crop bed preparation using a "turned pile" technique in which piles are formed with the appropriate ratios of nitrous, carbonous, and neutral materials, checked with a temperature gauge, and turned by-hand at weekly intervals.[^71] [^72]

## Uses

### Agriculture and gardening

![Compost - detail](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Compost_steaming_-_detail_3.jpg/250px-Compost_steaming_-_detail_3.jpg)

Compost used as fertilizer

On open ground for growing [wheat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheat "Wheat"), [corn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maize "Maize"), [soybeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soybean "Soybean"), and [similar crops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_crop "Row crop"), compost can be broadcast across the top of the soil using spreader trucks or spreaders pulled behind a tractor. It is expected that the spread layer is very thin (approximately 6 mm (0.24 in)) and worked into the soil prior to planting. Application rates of 25 mm (0.98 in) or more are not unusual when trying to rebuild poor soils or control erosion.[^73] In countries such as Germany, where compost distribution and spreading are partially subsidized in the original waste fees, compost is used more frequently on open ground on the premise of nutrient "sustainability".[^74]

In [plasticulture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticulture "Plasticulture"), [strawberries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strawberries "Strawberries"), [tomatoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tomato "Tomato"), [peppers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capsicum "Capsicum"), [melons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melon "Melon"), and other fruits and vegetables are grown under plastic to control temperature, retain moisture and control weeds. Compost may be banded (applied in strips along rows) and worked into the soil prior to bedding and planting, be applied at the same time the beds are constructed and plastic laid down, or used as a top dressing.

Many crops are not seeded directly in the field but are started in seed trays in a greenhouse. When the seedlings reach a certain stage of growth, they are [transplanted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transplanting "Transplanting") in the field. Compost may be part of the mix used to grow the seedlings, but is not normally used as the only planting substrate. The particular crop and the seeds' sensitivity to nutrients, salts, etc. dictates the ratio of the blend, and maturity is important to insure that oxygen deprivation will not occur or that no lingering phyto-toxins remain.[^75]

Compost can be added to soil, [coir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coir "Coir"), or [peat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peat "Peat"), as a [tilth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tilth "Tilth") improver, supplying humus and nutrients.[^76] It provides a rich growing medium as absorbent material. This material contains moisture and soluble minerals, which provide support and [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient "Nutrient"). Although it is rarely used alone, plants can flourish from mixed [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil") that includes a mix of compost with other additives such as [sand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand "Sand"), grit, bark chips, [vermiculite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vermiculite "Vermiculite"), [perlite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perlite "Perlite"), or [clay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clay "Clay") granules to produce [loam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loam "Loam"). Compost can be tilled directly into the soil or growing medium to boost the level of organic matter and the overall fertility of the soil. Compost that is ready to be used as an additive is dark brown or even black with an earthy smell.[^1] [^76]

Generally, direct seeding into a compost is not recommended due to the speed with which it may dry, the possible presence of [phytotoxins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytotoxin "Phytotoxin") in immature compost that may inhibit germination,[^77] [^78] [^79] and the possible tie up of nitrogen by incompletely decomposed lignin.[^80] It is very common to see blends of 20–30% compost used for transplanting [seedlings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seedlings "Seedlings").

Compost can be used to increase plant immunity to diseases and pests.[^81]

#### Compost tea

Compost tea is made up of extracts of fermented water leached from composted materials.[^76] [^82] Composts can be either aerated or non-aerated depending on its [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation "Fermentation") process.[^83] Compost teas are generally produced from adding compost to water in a ratio of 1:4–1:10, occasionally stirring to release [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbes "Microbes").[^83]

There is debate about the benefits of aerating the mixture.[^82] Non-aerated compost tea is cheaper and less labor-intensive, but there are conflicting studies regarding the risks of [phytotoxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytotoxicity "Phytotoxicity") and human pathogen regrowth.[^83] Aerated compost tea brews faster and generates more microbes, but has potential for human pathogen regrowth, particularly when one adds additional nutrients to the mixture.[^83]

Field studies have shown the benefits of adding compost teas to crops due to organic matter input, increased nutrient availability, and increased microbial activity.[^76] [^82] They have also been shown to have a suppressive effect on plant pathogens [^84] and soil-borne diseases.[^83] The efficacy is influenced by a number of factors, such as the preparation process, the type of source the conditions of the brewing process, and the environment of the crops.[^83] Adding nutrients to compost tea can be beneficial for disease suppression, although it can trigger the regrowth of human pathogens like *[E. coli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escherichia_coli "Escherichia coli")* and *[Salmonella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salmonella "Salmonella").*[^83]

#### Compost extract

Compost extracts are unfermented or non-brewed extracts of leached compost contents dissolved in any solvent.[^83]

#### Commercial sale

Compost is sold as bagged potting mixes in garden centers and other outlets.[^85] [^76] This may include composted materials such as manure and peat but is also likely to contain loam, fertilizers, sand, grit, etc. Varieties include multi-purpose composts designed for most aspects of planting, [John Innes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Innes_compost "John Innes compost") formulations,[^85] grow bags, designed to have crops such as tomatoes directly planted into them. There are also a range of specialist composts available, e.g. for vegetables, orchids, houseplants, hanging baskets, roses, ericaceous plants, seedlings, potting on, etc.[^86] [^87]

#### Community composting

Community composting uses organic materials sourced from a local community. Sites used for compost piles include educational institutions or [community gardens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_gardening "Community gardening"). Community composting programs may be involved in community education. The compost generated is used locally.[^88] Each community composting operation is generally smaller than a commercial composting operation.[^89]

### Other

Compost can also be used for land and stream reclamation, [wetland construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructed_wetland "Constructed wetland"), and [landfill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landfill "Landfill") cover.[^90]

The temperatures generated by compost can be used to heat [greenhouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse "Greenhouse"), such as by being placed around the outside edges.[^91]

## Regulations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Kitchen_compost_bin_-_Sarah_Stierch.jpg/250px-Kitchen_compost_bin_-_Sarah_Stierch.jpg)

A kitchen compost bin is used to transport compostable items to an outdoor compost bin.

There are process and product guidelines in Europe that date to the early 1980s (Germany, the Netherlands, Switzerland) and only more recently in the UK and the US. In both these countries, private trade associations within the industry have established loose standards, some say as a stop-gap measure to discourage independent government agencies from establishing tougher consumer-friendly standards.[^92] Compost is regulated in Canada [^93] and Australia [^94] as well.

EPA Class A and B guidelines in the United States [^95] were developed solely to manage the processing and [beneficial reuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beneficial_reuse "Beneficial reuse") of [sludge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sludge "Sludge"), also now called [biosolids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosolids "Biosolids"), following the US EPA ban of ocean dumping. About 26 American states now require composts to be processed according to these federal protocols for [pathogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen") and [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_\(epidemiology\) "Vector (epidemiology)") control, even though the application to non-sludge materials has not been scientifically tested. An example is that green waste composts are used at much higher rates than sludge composts were ever anticipated to be applied at.[^96] U.K guidelines also exist regarding compost quality,[^97] as well as Canadian,[^98] Australian,[^99] and the various European states.[^100]

In the United States, some compost manufacturers participate in a testing program offered by a private lobbying organization called the U.S. Composting Council. The USCC was originally established in 1991 by Procter & Gamble to promote composting of disposable diapers, following state mandates to ban diapers in landfills, which caused a national uproar. Ultimately the idea of composting diapers was abandoned, partly since it was not proven scientifically to be possible, and mostly because the concept was a marketing stunt in the first place. After this, composting emphasis shifted back to recycling organic wastes previously destined for landfills. There are no bonafide quality standards in America, but the USCC sells a seal called "Seal of Testing Assurance" [^101] (also called "STA"). For a considerable fee, the applicant may display the USCC logo on products, agreeing to volunteer to customers a current laboratory analysis that includes parameters such as nutrients, respiration rate, salt content, pH, and limited other indicators.[^102]

Many countries such as [Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wales "Wales") [^103] [^104] and some individual cities such as Seattle and [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Mandatory_Recycling_and_Composting_Ordinance "San Francisco Mandatory Recycling and Composting Ordinance") require food and yard waste to be sorted for composting ([San Francisco Mandatory Recycling and Composting Ordinance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Mandatory_Recycling_and_Composting_Ordinance "San Francisco Mandatory Recycling and Composting Ordinance")).[^105] [^106]

The USA is the only Western country that does not distinguish sludge-source compost from green-composts, and by default 50% of US states expect composts to comply in some manner with the federal EPA 503 rule promulgated in 1984 for sludge products.[^107]

There are health risk concerns about [PFASs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PFASs "PFASs") (" [forever chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forever_chemicals "Forever chemicals") ") levels in compost derived from sewage sledge sourced biosolids, and [EPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EPA "EPA") has not set health risk standards for this. The [Sierra Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Club "Sierra Club") recommends that home gardeners avoid the use of sewage sludge-base fertilizer and compost, in part due to potentially high levels of PFASs.[^108] The EPA **PFAS Strategic Roadmap** initiative, running from 2021 to 2024, will consider the full lifecycle of PFAS including health risks of PFAS in wastewater sludge.[^109]

## History

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Compost_Basket.jpg/250px-Compost_Basket.jpg)

Compost basket

Composting dates back to at least the early Roman Empire and was mentioned as early as [Cato the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cato_the_Elder "Cato the Elder") 's 160 BCE piece *[De Agri Cultura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Agri_Cultura "De Agri Cultura")*.[^110] Traditionally, composting involved piling organic materials until the next planting season, at which time the materials would have decayed enough to be ready for use in the soil. Methodologies for organic composting were part of [traditional agricultural systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_agriculture "Traditional agriculture") around the world.

Composting began to modernize somewhat in the 1920s in Europe as a tool for [organic farming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_farming "Organic farming").[^111] The first industrial station for the transformation of urban organic materials into compost was set up in [Wels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wels "Wels"), Austria, in the year 1921.[^112] Early proponents of composting in farming include [Rudolf Steiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Steiner "Rudolf Steiner"), founder of a farming method called [biodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodynamic_agriculture "Biodynamic agriculture"), and [Annie Francé-Harrar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annie_Franc%C3%A9-Harrar "Annie Francé-Harrar"), who was appointed on behalf of the government in [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico "Mexico") and supported the country in 1950–1958 to set up a large humus organization in the fight against erosion and [soil degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_degradation "Soil degradation").[^113] [Sir Albert Howard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Albert_Howard "Sir Albert Howard"), who worked extensively in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") on sustainable practices,[^111] and [Lady Eve Balfour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lady_Eve_Balfour "Lady Eve Balfour") were also major proponents of composting. Modern scientific composting was imported to America by the likes of [J. I. Rodale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._I._Rodale "J. I. Rodale") – founder of [Rodale, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodale,_Inc. "Rodale, Inc.") Organic Gardening, and others involved in the organic farming movement.[^111]

## India plays the major role in the development of compost and natural fertilizers.

India plays a significant role in the development and use of compost and natural fertilizers, supported by its long agricultural tradition and increasing emphasis on sustainable farming practices. Traditional techniques such as composting crop residues, animal manure, and organic waste have been widely practiced for centuries to maintain soil fertility and ecological balance. In recent decades, national initiatives like the National Mission for Sustainable Agriculture and the Paramparagat Krishi Vikas Yojana have actively promoted organic farming and the use of bio-fertilizers across the country.[^114]

In addition to government support, leading companies such as Coromandel International, National Fertilizers Limited, and Rashtriya Chemicals & Fertilizers have contributed to the research, production, and distribution of eco-friendly fertilizers, including compost-based and bio-organic products. India has also emerged as a key exporter of organic fertilizers and soil enhancers, supplying products such as vermicompost and bio-fertilizers to regions in Asia, Europe, and North America. This growing export capacity highlights India’s importance in the global transition toward sustainable and environmentally responsible agriculture.[^115]
