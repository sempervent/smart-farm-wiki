# Multi-field crop rotation plan (template)

**Purpose**: A **repeatable planning pattern** for splitting a farm into **several fields** (management units) and assigning **multi-year rotations** so cash crops, **cover crops**, and **livestock integration** do not all stress the same biology in the same place every year.

**Disclaimer**: This is a **generic template**, not agronomic advice for a specific parcel. Confirm **pests, diseases, organic matter, drainage, and markets** with **soil tests**, **local extension**, and your own records. Related time/labor framing: [`Timing on the farm — synthesis`](timing-on-the-farm-synthesis.md).

## 1. Define fields (management units)

Treat a “field” as any zone you manage **as a block**—fence line to fence line, or a set of beds that share tillage and fertility.

| Field ID | Suggested role | Notes |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| **Field A** | **Intensive annuals** — vegetables, herbs, cut flowers | Highest labor; benefit from **clear family rotation** and winter covers |
| **Field B** | **Mechanized annuals** — corn, beans, sorghum, sunflowers, field-scale veg | Machinery passes; good place for **green manures** between cash crops |
| **Field C** | **Soil-building / flex** — full-season covers, grazing, or “recovery” year | Use when another field is pushed hard; builds organic matter |
| **Field D** | **Perennials** — orchard, cane berries, asparagus, rhubarb | **No classical row-crop rotation**; manage **alley/understory** and nutrition instead |

Add or merge units to match your map (e.g. two distant parcels → **North** vs **South** rotations).

### 1a. Acreage allocation — “maximum production” in practice

**“Maximum production”** here means **highest farm-level sustainable throughput**—tons or revenue over the **whole farm** and **multiple years**—not maximizing one crop on every acre (which burns out soil and often wastes water). Soil organic matter and rotation **raise yield stability**; ingested irrigation notes stress **application efficiency** and **water storage** as co-limits ([`Irrigation and water-wise farming sources`](../topics/irrigation-and-water-wise-farming-sources.md)).

Use **Field C** (soil-building) as **~20–30%** of annual cropland in a given year where possible: that range trades some short-run acreage for **long-run** yield and drought resilience. **Field A** stays **small** unless labor, **irrigation**, and markets support more intensive acres.

| Example total farm acres | Field A (intensive) | Field B (mechanized annuals) | Field C (soil-building / flex) | Field D (perennials) | Notes |
|--------------------------|--------------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|----------------------|-------|
| **20** | **2** (10%) | **10** (50%) | **5** (25%) | **3** (15%) | Tight labor: keep A modest; C at **25%** for recovery |
| **40** | **4** (10%) | **22** (55%) | **10** (25%) | **4** (10%) | Scaled “general diversified” mix |
| **120** | **10** (~8%) | **72** (60%) | **28** (~23%) | **10** (~8%) | B dominates when **equipment** amortizes; **+2 ac to D** if tree/cane enterprise is a profit center |

**How to adjust**

- **Push more acres to B** when commodity markets, drill/planter width, and grain/logistics favor row crops.
- **Grow A** only when **water delivery** (drip, timers, pond pump) and **sales channels** can absorb the labor spike.
- **Raise C** after compaction, new ground, or fertility debt; **lower C** briefly if land debt is repaid and markets demand more cash acres—then monitor soil tests.

## 2. Crop families (rotation “gears”)

Avoid repeating the **same family** on the same ground every year. Common groupings for planning:

| Family (examples) | Wait before returning (rule of thumb) |
|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Solanaceae** (tomato, potato, pepper, eggplant) | **3–4 years** |
| **Brassicaceae** (broccoli, kale, turnip, radish) | **2–3 years** |
| **Cucurbitaceae** (squash, cucumber, melon) | **2–3 years** |
| **Fabaceae** (bean, pea, clover, vetch) | Often **beneficial** annually or every other year (N fixation)—still rotate **with** other families |
| **Alliums** (onion, garlic, leek) | **2–3 years** |
| **Apiaceae** (carrot, celery, parsley) | **2–3 years** |

## 3. Four-field coordinated plan (annuals + soil-building)

The tables below assume **Fields A–C** are in **annual production** and **Field D** is perennial or long-term sod (see §5). **Years** are a rolling cycle—**Year 5 = Year 1** again if the sequence still fits your markets and equipment.

### Field A — intensive vegetables / flowers

| Year | Spring / summer | Fall / winter |
|------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1 | **Solanaceae** (tomato, pepper) + companion / mulch system you use | **Cover**: oats + peas or crimson clover (kill before spring solanums if disease-sensitive) |
| 2 | **Brassicaceae** (broccoli, kale) + quick summer catch crop if timing allows | **Cover**: rye + vetch or mixed winter cover |
| 3 | **Fabaceae** (snap beans, bush beans) → **Cucurbitaceae** (summer squash) | **Cover**: winter rye or triticale |
| 4 | **Alliums** (onion family) or **Apiaceae** (carrots, parsnips) | **Cover**: grass–legume mix; consider **full soil rest** if compaction was high |

### Field B — mechanized row / bulk annuals

| Year | Main crop | Follow / winter |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Corn** or **sorghum** (grain or silage) | **Rye** or **mixed small grain** cover; or double-crop **wheat** if your season fits |
| 2 | **Soybeans** or **cowpeas** / dry beans | **Cereal rye** or **clover** overseed |
| 3 | **Small grain** (wheat, oats) for grain or straw | **Summer cover** (buckwheat, sudangrass) if window allows |
| 4 | **Field vegetables** (e.g. sweet potato, winter squash) or **sunflower** | **Legume-heavy cover** going into Year 1 |

### Field C — soil-building / recovery

| Year | Plan |
|------|------|
| 1 | **Full-season cover cocktail** (grasses + legumes + deep root species per your cover-crop guide) |
| 2 | **High-biomass summer** (sorghum-sudan, millet) → winter **rye/vetch** |
| 3 | **Pasture or hay** if livestock integrated; else **mowed green manure** |
| 4 | **Short-season cash** (e.g. pumpkins, grain) only if soil tests justify; otherwise continue **covers** |

**Farm-level idea**: When **Field A** is in a heavy **solanaceous** year, keep **Field B** away from potatoes/tomatoes in the same year if **shared equipment** moves soilborne pathogens—rinse or sanitize tools between fields.

## 5. Field D — perennials (rotation of practices, not crops)

Perennial blocks do not rotate like corn and beans. Instead plan:

- **Alley floor**: low-growing **clover**, **mowed sod**, or **annual ryegrass** between rows; avoid herbicide patterns that damage trees.
- **Nutrition**: soil and leaf tests; organic mulches vs synthetic programs per your certification.
- **Pest/disease**: sanitation, pruning airflow, **understory diversity** where compatible with your system.

If **Field D** is **annual** land in transition, run it through **Field C** style covers for 1–2 years before planting perennials.

## 6. One-page annual checklist

1. **Map** each field’s planned **family** and **cover**.
2. **Order seed** and **cover crop seed** together.
3. **Log** actual plantings (date, variety, field)—rotation fails without records.
4. **Soil test** at least on a rotation (e.g. every 3 years per field or by soil type).
5. **Adjust** next year if a disease appeared (lengthen family break).
